Missing the Forest for the Trees

I always start writing hot on the tails of some flare of an idea, with no guarantee that it'll work out. Here's this one:

Discussing Object-Oriented Programming versus Functional Programming is missing the forest for the trees

I'm spurred on by some (quite interesting) work by Ted Kaminski. In the linked post, he makes a distinction between "data" and "objects", based on the following five characteristics:

  • Equality and Identity: are two values, say 23857 and 23857 always the same, or can they have a different identity?
  • Mutability: can I turn this 23857 into 23856, or not?
  • Abstraction: can I hide the details of how Thing is implemented, or is Thing just data?
  • Extensibility: do I have a fixed set of operations for this Thing (that I might be able to evolve through inheritance), or can I write whatever methods?

I grouped equality and identity because they are the same: a notion of equality induces a notion of identity. Things are identical (have the same identity) if they are equal. The question is whether there is only one notion of equality or multiple. In some languages identity is by-value, i.e. two values are the same if they are bit-for-bit equal. In others identity is by-place, i.e. two values are the same thing only if they are at the same spot in memory.

Places and values

I think this points at a better distinction: places and values. At the very basis of it, most programming is operating on bits in memory and transforming them. The processing unit reads bits from memory, does some calculations on them, then writes them back to memory. Hopefully the bits it wrote go on to do some good in the world, like showing a nice picture on your computer screen or the answer to your mathematics homework. To make sure the right bits are written to the right locations, it's essential to know what (value) these bits are and where (place) they are.

Programming languages are mostly way of making the problem of dealing with reading, transforming, and writing the right bits from the right spot tractable for humans. Some ideas that help: encoding higher-level notions as bits, and working with those higher-level notions. Things like ASCII (00001010 means a newline \n), or floating point numbers, or even integers. (This is what people mean when they say "C is a high-level language".)

"Data" in the distinction between "data" and "objects" has a clear story in terms of places and values: the notion of place is completely managed by the programming language. When you program, you only concern yourself with the values and their transformation. It doesn't matter whether your first 23857 is at the same place your second 23857. The programming language (compiler/interpreter/runtime) will take care of it.

"Objects", on the other hand, seem to not have a clear story. Objects do have a place (i.e. identity), but this is almost incidental.

The distinctions are not very clearly explained in the text, and I think they are muddy at best. Coming from Python, a method on a class is just a function taking an instance of that class. This is completely the same as extensibility in the "data"-column. But methods are also overrideable. This is conflating where calculations are defined with where they can be accessed.

I think there are only two fundamental distinctions between the columns: that of notions of equality, and encapsulation (can you hide things).

In regular programming, values are data, and places are references, or pointers, or addresses.
